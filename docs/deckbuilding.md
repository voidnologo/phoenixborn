# Deckbuilding Guide

Deckbuilding in Ashes is less about finding one broken card and more about assembling a small, self-consistent machine: a Phoenixborn, a set of dice that reliably powers the cards you draw, and thirty cards that all want to do the same thing. Here is a method that works.

## The four-step method

The community's foundational deckbuilding article ("Colorful Cubes," by Jayson Seth Lindley) boils the process down to four steps, and it's a genuinely good spine to build on:

1. **Choose your Phoenixborn.** The Phoenixborn defines everything downstream: their **battlefield size** (how many units can be in play), their **life total**, their **spellboard limit** (how many Ready Spells you can have out), and above all their **ability**, which you can use every single round for free-or-cheap. Build the deck to feed that ability.
2. **Decide which magic (dice) types to use.** Most decks commit to **two or three** magic types. This is the single most important structural decision, because it determines what your dice can pay for.
3. **Select cards that align with those types.** Anchor on your chosen types' best removal and best units, then add support and reaction spells.
4. **Refine through testing.** No amount of theory replaces playing the deck and watching where it stalls. Expect to swap three to six cards after your first few games.

## The seven magic types

Every card and every die belongs to a magic type. Each type has a distinct "die power" (the effect you can trigger by spending a die on its power side) and a distinct personality. Learning these is most of the battle.

| Type | Die power (the free tool it gives you) | What it's good at | Signature cards |
|---|---|---|---|
| ![Ceremonial](assets/dice/ceremonial.png){.die title="Ceremonial"} **Ceremonial** | Return an ally from your discard pile | Recursion, sacrifice loops, relentless aggression and burn | Final Cry, Fester, Summon Sleeping Widows, Fire Archer |
| ![Charm](assets/dice/charm.png){.die title="Charm"} **Charm** | Reduce an enemy unit's attack | Neutralizing attackers, tempo, focused combo pieces | Golden Veil, Hypnotize, Summon Three-Eyed Owl, Beast Tamer |
| ![Illusion](assets/dice/illusion.png){.die title="Illusion"} **Illusion** | Downgrade two of the opponent's dice | Disruption, denial, reaction spells, enabling swarms | Gates Thrown Open, Particle Shield, Summon Shadow Spirit, To Shadows |
| ![Natural](assets/dice/natural.png){.die title="Natural"} **Natural** | Deal 1 damage to a unit | Efficient removal and solid, aggressively-priced bodies | Molten Gold, Nature's Wrath, Ice Trap, Summon Frostback Bear |
| ![Divine](assets/dice/divine.png){.die title="Divine"} **Divine** | Increase a friendly unit's attack | Big board presence, buffs, effects that dictate play | Meteor, Summon Light Bringer, Heal, Sword of Virtue |
| ![Sympathy](assets/dice/sympathy.png){.die title="Sympathy"} **Sympathy** | Draw a card | Flexibility, card advantage, adaptable tactics | Crescendo, Sympathy Pain, Summon Salamander Monk, Magic Syphon |
| ![Time](assets/dice/time.png){.die title="Time"} **Time** | Manipulate status tokens (add / move / spend) | Bending the action economy, exhaustion control, efficient removal | Summon Time Hopper, Accelerate, Chaos Gravity, To Shadows |

!!! note "About the colors and the two newest types"
    Those are the game's **actual dice symbols**, in their real colors — the same icons you'll see on the dice and on [ashes.live](https://ashes.live). Ascendancy adds two more types — **Astral** (bee) and **Artifice** (owl), used by Arren Frostpeak and Issa Brightmore — which are their own ecosystem and aren't used by the decks here.

A quick way to internalize the personalities: **Natural** is the honest, brutal foundation — great removal, great creatures, no tricks needed. **Ceremonial** is mean and recursive and closes games fast. **Illusion** wins by denying the opponent rather than building a board. **Divine** overwhelms with sheer size and forces the table to react. **Charm** asks a lot of the pilot but breaks games open. **Sympathy** keeps your hand full so you always have an answer. **Time** rewrites the normal flow of turns.

## Choosing and balancing your dice

You may bring **up to ten dice**, split among your chosen magic types however you like. There is no single correct split — a clean **5/5**, a **4/3/3**, and a **4/4/2** all work — but a few rules of thumb keep a deck from choking:

- **Your dice must pay for your First Five on turn one.** This is the hard constraint. If your opening play needs two Natural symbols and a Charm symbol, your ten dice had better make that likely on the first roll. Build the dice around the opening, not the other way around.
- **Count your "class" pips.** Many strong cards demand a specific magic *class* symbol (not just any basic). If a card costs "2 Natural (class)," a Charm die can't help pay it. Give each color you rely on enough dice — usually **at least three or four** if that color carries key cards; **two** is fine for a light splash of one or two cheap cards.
- **More colors means less reliability.** Two colors is the most consistent. Three is the normal ceiling. Four is a stretch worth making only for a specific, cheap payoff — and expect to occasionally miss.
- **Dice do double duty.** A die you didn't spend on a card can still be spent for its **die power** or used as a **basic** symbol. A deck that leans on a color's die power — Natural's "1 damage," Charm's "-1 attack" — wants extra dice of that color beyond what its cards strictly demand.

!!! tip "A practical starting recipe"
    For most two-color decks, start at **6 of your primary color, 4 of your secondary**, then adjust after testing.

## Picking your First Five

Your **First Five** are the five cards you start the game holding; the other twenty-five form your draw deck. Experienced players consider this the most important deckbuilding decision in the game — a strong First Five can effectively decide a match inside the first round, and many players build the entire deck backward from the five cards they want to open with.

A good First Five usually wants to:

- **Establish your board or engine immediately.** Most decks open with one or two **Summon** (Ready Spell) cards so you have units and a working spellboard by the end of round one.
- **Be fully affordable on turn one.** If you can't reliably cast a card on the first turn, it probably shouldn't be in the opening hand. Count the dice.
- **Include a way to interact.** A piece of removal or a Reaction spell keeps you from getting run over by a faster start.
- **Set up the following round.** Cards that ramp your draw or lay the foundation for a round-two combo pay off enormously because you get to keep and build on them.

Avoid loading the First Five with expensive, situational, or purely reactive cards you can't use yet — those are better drawn into later.

## Balancing the thirty cards

Beyond dice and the opening hand, a healthy 30-card deck generally wants:

- **Enough units to hold the battlefield.** Between Allies and the conjurations your Summon spells produce, you need enough bodies to contest the (usually 4–6 slot) battlefield every round.
- **Ready Spells within your spellboard limit.** Your Phoenixborn can only host so many Ready Spells at once, and Summon spells are Ready Spells too — they occupy slots.
- **A real removal package.** You need repeatable answers — die powers, cheap removal spells, or combat units. Decks that can't kill anything lose to decks that can.
- **A sane curve.** Have things to do on turn one, strong plays on turns two and three, and not so many expensive cards that you sit with a full hand you can't pay for.
- **Reaction spells as insurance.** A couple of well-chosen reactions (Golden Veil, Particle Shield, Final Cry, Sympathy Pain) protect your key pieces and punish overextension without costing a main action.

## The most common beginner mistakes

!!! warning "Watch out for these"
    - **Too many colors, too few dice of each** — the deck looks powerful and plays inconsistently.
    - **A First Five that can't act on turn one** — you fall a full round behind and never recover.
    - **No removal** — every strong deck can clear a unit; if yours can't, you lose the board.
    - **Over-summoning past the battlefield or spellboard limit** — dead cards in hand.
    - **Ignoring the Phoenixborn's ability** — it's free value every round; build to use it constantly.
